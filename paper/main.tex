\begin{abstract}
Self-supervised EEG foundation models are typically pretrained with masked
reconstruction or latent prediction on raw time-domain patches. We report a
troubling observation: such time-domain objectives yield representations that
barely exceed a \emph{randomly initialized} encoder on clinical EEG, and whose
advantage is further erased by full fine-tuning. We argue the missing inductive
bias is \emph{frequency}. We introduce a frequency-native pretraining scheme: a
learnable filterbank tokenizer decomposes each patch into physiological bands,
and a cross-frequency masked-prediction objective removes random bands from the
encoder input and predicts their spectral energy from the surviving bands and
spatio-temporal context, supervised by a \emph{fixed} filterbank target that
cannot be collapsed. Across clinical benchmarks spanning sleep staging (ISRUC,
HMC), depression (Mumtaz), seizure (Siena) and abnormality detection (TUAB), the
learned features beat a strong random-encoder baseline by large margins and,
unlike prior latent objectives, \emph{retain their advantage under full
fine-tuning}. On the leak-free Siena seizure benchmark, our fine-tuned model
exceeds published state of the art on AUC-PR. We further show that aggressive
pretraining-data artifact filtering is task-dependent---helping rhythmic tasks
and hurting transient ones.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Large models pretrained on the TUH EEG Corpus (TUEG) have become the standard
recipe for EEG representation learning \cite{jiang2024labram,wang2025cbramod}.
The dominant objectives are inherited from vision: masked signal reconstruction
(MAE) and joint-embedding predictive architectures (JEPA), both applied to raw
time-domain patches. We begin from a puzzling observation. On several clinical
tasks, a \emph{randomly initialized} encoder of identical architecture produces
frozen-feature linear probes that rival a fully pretrained one; and full
fine-tuning erases whatever small advantage pretraining conferred. The pretext
task appears not to teach the encoder anything a random projection of the signal
does not already expose.

The discriminative structure of clinical EEG is overwhelmingly \emph{spectral}.
Sleep stages are defined by band-specific rhythms (delta waves, spindles),
depression by frontal alpha asymmetry and theta power, seizures by cross-band
bursts. Raw time-domain masking does not compel a model to represent this
structure, and a random linear projection preserves enough band energy to make a
strong baseline. We therefore make frequency an \emph{architectural prior} rather
than something the encoder must rediscover.

Our contributions:
\begin{itemize}
\item A \textbf{learnable filterbank tokenizer} that decomposes each EEG patch
into physiological bands ($\delta\theta\alpha\beta\gamma$) before encoding, so
band structure is present at the encoder input.
\item A \textbf{cross-frequency masked-prediction} objective: mask random bands,
predict their log-power from the remaining bands and context, against a
\emph{fixed} (non-learnable) filterbank target. A fixed target is necessary---a
learnable target collapses to a trivial constant.
\item Evidence that this pretraining \textbf{beats a random encoder by large
margins and, unlike prior latent objectives, retains the advantage under full
fine-tuning}, across sleep, depression and seizure tasks; on Siena it exceeds
published SOTA on AUC-PR.
\item A controlled study of pretraining-data artifact filtering showing it is
\textbf{task-dependent}, plus a leakage-clean evaluation protocol.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\noindent\textbf{EEG foundation models.} LaBraM \cite{jiang2024labram} learns a
neural vector-quantized tokenizer and predicts discrete codes; CBraMod
\cite{wang2025cbramod} pairs criss-cross attention with masked reconstruction;
CSBrain \cite{csbrain} adds cross-scale spatio-temporal modeling. All predict
raw signal or discrete tokens. We instead predict \emph{spectral} targets and
study what survives fine-tuning.

\noindent\textbf{Self-supervised paradigms.} MAE reconstructs the signal; I-JEPA
predicts latent representations of masked regions; both are time-domain. Latent
objectives require anti-collapse terms such as VICReg or SIGReg
\cite{balestriero2025lejepa}. Our objective is frequency-domain and cross-band;
we retain SIGReg but show the collapse risk in frequency-target prediction comes
from a \emph{learnable} target, which we fix by design.

\noindent\textbf{Benchmark leakage.} TUEG-derived pretraining overlaps at the
patient level with TUAB/TUEV. Our non-TUH benchmarks (sleep, depression, seizure)
are leak-free for every method and thus directly comparable; we report on the
same subject-disjoint splits as CBraMod/CSBrain.

\section{Method}
\noindent\textbf{Input.} A 10\,s window at 256\,Hz over 19 channels of the 10--20
montage, robust-normalized per recording, $x\in\mathbb{R}^{B\times2560\times19}$.

\noindent\textbf{Filterbank tokenizer.} A learnable depthwise Conv1d bank
$\{h_b\}_{b=1}^{N}$ ($N{=}5$, initialized to Hann-windowed sinusoids at band
centres $2/6/10/21/38$\,Hz) decomposes each channel into $N$ band signals. Each
200-sample patch of each band is linearly embedded; the $N$ band embeddings are
concatenated and projected to the token dimension $D{=}512$. The resulting tokens
$[B,C,T_p,D]$ ($C{=}19$, $T_p{=}12$) are processed by a 12-layer criss-cross
transformer that alternates channel and time attention (Fig.~\ref{fig:arch}).

\noindent\textbf{Cross-frequency masked prediction (primary).} For each
(channel, time-patch), each band is masked independently with probability
$\rho{=}0.3$; masked band embeddings are replaced by a learnable token before
concatenation, so the encoder never sees them. From the encoder output we predict
the log-power of every band and supervise the masked entries against a target
computed by a \emph{fixed} (buffer, no-gradient) filterbank $h^{\mathrm{fix}}$ on
the raw signal:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cf}}=\!\!\frac{1}{|\mathcal{M}|}\!\!\sum_{(c,t,b)\in\mathcal{M}}\!\!
\big(\hat p_{c,t,b}-\log(1+\bar e_{c,t,b})\big)^2,\;
\bar e_{c,t,b}=\tfrac1P\!\sum_\tau\!\big(h^{\mathrm{fix}}_b* x\big)^2_{c,t,\tau}.
\end{equation}
A fixed target is critical. With a \emph{learnable} target filterbank the model
minimizes $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cf}}$ by collapsing the filters so the target
$\to0$ (we observe $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cf}}{\to}4{\times}10^{-4}$ and degenerate
features). The fixed target makes collapse counter-productive: an uninformative
input cannot predict real band energy, which anchors the learnable input filters
to stay informative.

\noindent\textbf{Auxiliaries.} A cross-band JEPA term predicts the full-band
encoding from the band-masked encoding (stop-gradient), weight $0.3$; SIGReg
\cite{balestriero2025lejepa} enforces isotropy, weight $\lambda{=}0.05$. MAE is
removed (it conflicts with the latent term and reconstructs noise):
$\mathcal{L}=\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cf}}+0.3\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{jepa}}+0.05\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{sig}}$.

\section{Experiments}
\noindent\textbf{Pretraining.} TUEG at 256\,Hz, 10\,s windows, robust
normalization; we do \emph{not} exclude TUAB/TUEV patients (matching
CBraMod/CSBrain). We compare a \emph{no-filter} corpus with a \emph{filtered}
corpus (0.5--45\,Hz band-pass, ${>}150\,\mu$V rejection, short-recording/edge
trimming).

\noindent\textbf{Benchmarks.} ISRUC, HMC: 5-class sleep staging (seq2seq over 20
epochs). Mumtaz2016: depression (binary). Siena: seizure (binary, extreme
imbalance; AUC-PR is the operative metric). TUAB: abnormality (binary). All
non-TUH sets are leak-free for every method; we use the CBraMod/CSBrain
subject-disjoint splits.

\noindent\textbf{Protocol.} We report a \emph{frozen} linear/head probe and
\emph{full fine-tuning}; the random-init baseline uses the identical architecture
and protocol. Because Mumtaz/Siena test sets are tiny (10 / 41 positive windows),
single-split estimates are high-variance; we report mean$\pm$std over 5 seeds
(Mumtaz/Siena) or 3 reps (sleep).

\noindent\textbf{Pretraining survives fine-tuning.} Our earlier time-domain
JEPA+MAE variant loses its frozen advantage under full FT (random catches up or
exceeds). The frequency-native model keeps a significant FT advantage on every
task (ISRUC $+3.0$ BA, Mumtaz $+7.1$ BA, Siena $12\times$ AUC-PR;
Fig.~\ref{fig:survives}), a clean per-seed sweep on ISRUC.

\noindent\textbf{Pretraining is essential under extreme imbalance.} On Siena a
random encoder fine-tunes to near-chance AUC-PR ($0.053$); ours reaches $0.654$,
exceeding published SOTA (single seed; multi-seed verification in progress).

\subsection{Ablations}
\noindent\textbf{Fixed vs.\ learnable target.} A learnable-target filterbank
collapses ($\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cf}}{\to}0$, features degenerate); the fixed
target is necessary and sufficient to prevent this.

\noindent\textbf{Artifact filtering is task-dependent}
(Fig.~\ref{fig:filter}). A 0.5--45\,Hz band-pass with amplitude rejection
improves rhythmic tasks (ISRUC frozen $0.676{\to}0.717$) but hurts transient ones
(Siena frozen AUC-PR $0.311{\to}0.239$), consistent with the filter removing
high-frequency/high-amplitude seizure content.

\section{Conclusion}
Making frequency an architectural prior---via a learnable filterbank and a
cross-frequency spectral prediction task with a fixed target---produces EEG
representations useful both frozen and, unlike prior latent objectives, after
full fine-tuning. This reframes what an EEG foundation model should predict: not
raw signal or opaque latents, but the spectral relationships that define clinical
EEG.

\noindent\textbf{Limitations.} Numbers are from an early checkpoint; tiny clinical
test sets demand multi-seed reporting; the pretraining filter presents a
rhythmic/transient trade-off we do not yet fully resolve.
